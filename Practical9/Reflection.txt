I am really proud of myself after designing the logic of the code and running it successfully.
Through this exercise, I encountered several key problems that taught me important lessons about computational modeling. 
The first issue was my misunderstanding of how probability is implemented—I didn't understand why infection should be determined by `if random() < beta` rather than equality, until I realized that continuous random numbers almost never land exactly on a specific value. 
The second problem was a structural error in my 1D model: I used `p_Infected = beta * I / (N-Imm)`, incorrectly assuming that immune people should be excluded from the denominator. 
I learned that all contacts still occur, but immune individuals simply don't get infected, so the denominator must always be the total population. 
The third issue was negative population values—my code tried to infect more people than were available. 
Switching from `np.random.choice()` to `np.random.binomial()` solved this because the binomial function  respects population limits. 
In the 2D model, I initially updated the population while iterating through infected individuals, which caused chain infections within the same time step. 
I learned to record changes in separate lists (`new_infections`, `recoveries`) and apply them in batch after processing all infected cells. 
Finally, edge cells in the grid had fewer than eight neighbors, so I added boundary checks before accessing neighbor coordinates.
